What's This About?

For our Catalyst assignment in Chemistry 12, we wanted to look at how rain is formed through the process of nucleation! There have been many attempts throughout history to successfully create rain and snow artificially through cloud seeding, with mixed results. To properly “make” rain, you have to have a catalyst. The most common catalysts we see in nature for this are aerosol catalysts, namely calcium, aluminium, or magnesium. These catalysts float through the air and attract water particles to them, and when they have collected enough particles and have joined with enough others, they will make rain. However, according to new research, tiny bio-particles or microbes may have a higher influence on the nucleation of raindrops than we first thought. These aerosole microbes are known as ice-nucleation active bacteria since they most typically influence snow and hail rather than rain.

What is Nucleation?

Nucleation is the initial process that occurs during the formation of a object, such as a rain drop. A bunch of small ions, molecules or atoms arrange themselves in a pattern. These often congregate around a central point, growing in size. This can begin from liquid or a vapour, and it binds itself to form a raindrop or a crystal etc.

What is a Catalyst?

A catalyst is a substance that speeds up or increases a chemical reaction, but doesn’t go through any change itself. In this case, our catalyst is what makes it possible for rain to form.

So how does rain actually form?

To artificially nucleate raindrops, the most usual catalyst used is silver iodide. The reason that we don’t see more artificially created rain is because silver iodide is known to be toxic to human and animals. The reaction of rain is water vapour (g) + heat → water/rain (l). The reason rain nucleation makes rain instead of just clouds is that when too many rain particles stick together, it gets heavier and heavier slowly, until eventually gravity takes over and the water has to start falling. If the temperature in the cloud is below zero, we will get snow, hail, or freezing rain instead of normal rain. This is also how acid rain is formed, but it includes compounds like sulphur dioxide and nitrogen oxide being released into the air and reacting with the water particles, and forming acidic pollutants more commonly known as acid rain. If there is a sandstorm somewhere in the world, (most likely large deserts) the wind will pick up the sand and carry it far across the earth, collecting water particles as they go. Once they have travelled far enough and collected enough water, it will create rain. The more sand that flies around, the bigger the rain storm is, since there are more catalysts to react with.
